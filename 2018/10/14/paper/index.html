Fundamental Analysis of Idiosyncratic Volatility Puzzle in China

By Hongye Fang [^mark1]

[^mark1]: 2016 Econ, 1601213422. I hereby give thanks to Prof.David Ong's comments and suggestions and Prof.Changyong Ha for pointing my previous mistake.

Abstract

The negative correlation between idiosyncratic volatility and expected returns in the stock market has been debated for decades. This paper is the first one adopts Ang et al.(2006) 's method and extends their analysis to financial accounting factors with different weighting regime in China's stock market. This paper confirms existence of idiosyncratic volatility puzzle and further finds that size, momentum can not allay the magnitude of puzzle,while pe, pb and ps ratio can reduce the degree of puzzle, nevertheless equally weighting does not change the result for most factors I tested. Nevertheless, for large pe stocks(tend to be overpriced) IVOL puzzle disappear, which is consistent with Stambaugh Yu and Yuan (2015) 's theory.

Key words: Idiosyncratic volatility puzzle, anomalies, asset pricing, China stock market

1. Introduction

The foundation of modern asset pricing theory has been well established by endeavor of the former economists, however, there are more spaces worth exploration. Idiosyncratic Volatility Puzzle, highlighted by Ang, Hordrick, Xing and Zhang(2006), is one of basic problems that attracted both empirics and theorists.

As CAPM shows, it is systematic risk that matters rather than non-systematic risk such as idiosyncratic risk (identified as the volatility of residuals of CAPM equation $\tilde{R_{i,t}}= \alpha_i + \beta_i \tilde{R_{m,t}} + u_{i,t}$) due to market wide diversification. i.e. Idiosyncratic volatility is irrelevant to stock returns, because investors can always diversify their risk via portfolio management. Obviously, as what Shefrin and Statman(1985) found, let alone people are not welling to short sell (therefore it is hard for investors to construct a portfolio with negative weighted asset), both individual and institutional investors are not even well diversified. Similarly, argued by Merton(1987), investors should take idiosyncratic risk into account. According to Merton's theory, idiosyncratic risk should be positively correlate with expected returns at cross-section level. However, empirical findings are very confusing : Lintner(1965), Tinic and West (1986), Lehmann (1990), Malkiel and Xu(2002), and Fu (2009) found that idiosyncratic volatility is positively correlated with expected returns, but Ang et al. (2006), Chen et al. (2012) found robust negative correlation. Interestingly, there is no theory can explain such negative relationship. One notable finding is Stambaugh Yu and Yuan (2015), who argued that the correlation between idiosyncratic volatility and expected returns varies among different mispriced stocks via the channel of arbitrage asymmetry.

Mainly based on Stambaugh et al. (2015)'s idea, I propose to investigate into following questions in China Stock Markets: Does idiosyncratic volatility (IVOL) puzzle exist in china? What is the difference between short selling restricted market and arbitrage asymmetry market in terms of mispricing? The remainder of the proposal is organized as follows: section 2 makes a comprehensive review of what the academia reached in terms of idiosyncratic volatility puzzle; section 3 introduces my approach to address the problems I put forward in the beginning; section 4 is my primitive findings.

2. Literature Review

In this Section, I summarize former researches from two aspects: first, the relationship between idiosyncratic volatility puzzle and behavioral finance; second, the researches of IVOL puzzle in China stock markets.

a. Idiosyncratic Volatility Puzzle and Behavioral Finance

Fama and French (1993) show that the CAPM can be extend to a model with three factors: the market factor, a factor based on market equity (small-minus-big, SMB), and a factor based on book-to-market equity (high-minus-low, HML). Carhart(1997) raise 4-factor model in capturing the remaining significant anomalies. Turan G. Bali and Nusret Cakici (2008) study idiosyncratic volatility and the cross section of expected returns. They indicate that the size, price, and liquidity play critical roles in determining the existence and significance of a relation between idiosyncratic risk and the cross section of expected returns. Hou, Xue and Zhang (2015) constructed a new empirical model that largely summarizes the cross section of average stock returns. This paper builds a new workhorse model on these effects for the cross section of expected stock returns and compares the performance of major empirical asset pricing models. Many seemingly unrelated anomalies turn out to be different manifestations of the investment and profitability effects.

By discovering that many market anomalies share common characteristics, Stambaugh et al. (2015) create a framework of analyzing mispricing and market anomalies. In their paper, they use average ranking of 11 anomalies well documented in previous papers to give a relative measurement for each of the stock and then found that positive correlation between idiosyncratic volatility showed in the most underpriced stocks while negative correlation showed in the most overpriced stocks. Their findings consistent with other researches that the overall correlation is negative due to pervasive overpricing. Moreover, their model firstly attribute such phenomenon to arbitrage asymmetry.

To show the behavioral explanation, Stambaugh et al. (2015) assumed that there are three different kinds of investors, who are Mean-variance investors, hedge-fund and noise traders. The idea is that for a given level of IVOL, the demands of noise traders can cause a relatively greater effect on equilibrium alpha when those demands go in the direction of producing overpricing as opposed to underpricing. The noise traders ,according to assumption, cannot be free for short-selling of all the assets, which is just like a common investor in real life. Then they assumed the noise traders can short sell $N_1$ kind of assets out of all the N kinds of assets. For each asset i, and $N$ grows large with $N_1$,the derived the form of $\alpha_i$:

$$\alpha_i = \mu_i - \beta_i \mu_m$$

and they derived the relationship between idiosyncratic volatility $\sigma_{\epsilon,i}$ and excessive demand for stock i ($y_i$) form noisy traders as following(other terms is constant), if the investors in group $I_m$(constrained group) have a non-zero position in stock i, then:

$$\alpha_i = Ay_i\frac{\sigma^2_{\epsilon,i}}{M+H}$$

If the investors in group$I_m$ have a zero position in stock i, then:

$$\alpha_i = Ay_i\frac{\sigma^2_{\epsilon,i}}{H}$$

Henceforth, for a given level of IVOL, the demands of noise traders can cause a relatively greater effect on equilibrium alpha when those demands go in the direction of producing overpricing as opposed to underpricing. Due to the arbitrage asymmetry, for a given level of excess noise-trader who have positive demand for underpriced stocks, abnormal returns and IVOL have a positive relation, whereas the relationship is negative for overpriced stocks with a given negative demand. Therefore, if the negative relation among overpriced stocks is stronger, the overall IVOL-return relation is negative. Based on their theory, I come up with idea that IVOL puzzle must share some features with other mispricing measurements, such as financial factors.

b. IVOL Puzzle in China Stock Markets

Li, Shi and Yu(2014) used rolling-monthly realized idiosyncratic volatility and the cross-sectional regression method to test whether there is unusual phenomenon of Chinese stock market. The result shows that Chinese stock markets have negative correlation of cross-section return and volatility and the phenomenon proves there are characteristics of China’s stock market idiosyncratic volatility puzzle. Xiong and Chen (2015), find that stock IVOL is positively correlated with stock return and the elasticity of stock return toward IVOL is increasing as investor sentiment and noisy investors proportion increases, this is based on 2007-2012 A-share listed companies. However, Yu, Zhang and Zhao (2017), find the negative correlation between stock return and stock IVOL, based on 1996-2015 A-share 2698 listed companies. They also propose that short selling can alleviate the level while the negative correlation is still significant.

Yet all of the researches of China's IVOL puzzle do not adopt a control of IVOL among stocks, neither they apply any comprehensive and dynamic analysis together with other anomalies. Fu liu and Li(2015) apply a static analysis controlling financial quality, they find that firms with better quality tend to have less abnormal return using IVOL strategy. But their analysis do not reflect the comprehensive relationship with financial quality and IVOL puzzle and their data frequency is quarterly. My analysis takes financial report data and mispricing into account, therefore pe is most easy one to apply. Pe ratio usually used to measure if one stock is overpriced in relative magnitude.

3. Empirical Approach

My empirical approach consist three parts: How to measure idiosyncratic volatility? How to design an empirical experiment to prove the existence of IVOL puzzle? How to test the interaction between IVOL and other factors?

a. Measuring Idiosyncratic Volatility

My idiosyncratic volatility measurement is based on Ang et al.(2006), who use daily data and run regression for each stock i within every month m:

$$R_{i,t} = \alpha_{i,m} + \beta_{i,m} MKT_t + \gamma_{i,m} SMB_t + \delta_{i,m} HML_t + \epsilon_{i,t}$$

where i denotes stock, t denotes time(daily) and m denotes time(monthly), $N_{i,m}$ denotes number of trading days in month m.

keep $\epsilon_ {i,t}$ for stock i in each month m, which has a trading period larger than 15 days, IVOL for stock i is defined as $IVOL_{i,m} = \sqrt{N_{i,m}}\sigma_{i,m}(\epsilon_{i,t})$. The consideration here is that Ang et al.(2006) is the origin of idiosyncratic volatility puzzle, it would be more consistent to following their method than using other methods. More over, Stambaugh et al. (2015) adopt this approach as well and they argue that Fu(2009) using a rolling Garch model suffers from a potential 'looking forward' bias.

b. Verifying Idiosyncratic Volatility Puzzle

I intend to apply a staggered verification of idiosyncratic volatility puzzle: Fama-Macbeth regression and Ang et al(2006)'s 5 times 5 sorting approach. The first step is Fama-Macbeth regression using idiosyncratic volatility of time t to predict expected returns of time t+1 :

$$R_{i,m+1} = \alpha_m + \beta_t IVOL_{i,m} + \epsilon_{i,m}$$

where $R_{i,m+1}$ denotes return of stock i and time(month) m+1, $IVOL_{i,m}$ is idiosyncratic volatility of stock i at month m and $\epsilon_{i,m}$ denotes residual. This equation is basically a cross-section regression, therefore, I use this method to check the overall relationship at each cross-section.

The second method comes from Ang et al(2006) and no one adopt it in China until now. Their approach includes following steps:

  • step1 Rank all the stocks at month m according to each of their idiosyncratic volatility at month m, divide them into 5 layers, use high IVOL, NEXT 20%, NEXT 20%, NEXT 20% and low IVOL to denote each layer;

  • step2 Construct portfolios in each layer and then form a new portfolio by using return of low IVOL portfolio minus high IVOL portfolio both value weighted and equally weighted,and update them every month;

  • step3 I then can get time series value and equally weighted return of five portfolios via step1 and step2, run following regression:

    $$R_{i,m} = \alpha_{i} + \beta_{i} MKT_m + \gamma_{i} SMB_m + \delta_{i} HML_m + \epsilon_{i,m} $$

    where $R_{i,m}$ denotes return of portfolio i (range from high IVOL, NEXT 20%, NEXT 20%, NEXT 20% to low IVOL) and month m, $MKT_m, SMB_m and HML_m$ are Fama-French three factor model at month m and $\epsilon_{i,m}$ denotes residual.

  • step4 Calculate $\alpha_i$ in step3, constructing Newey-West robust standard error for each portfolio, verifying if $\alpha_i$ is positive and significant different from 0.

c. Verifying Idiosyncratic Volatility Puzzle and Potential Factors

In this part, I extend the sorting method into two-dimension to investigate in where single factor such as Size and help explain idiosyncratic volatility puzzle.

  • step1 Rank all the stocks at month m according to each of their size, divide them into 5 layers, use large cap, NEXT 20%, NEXT 20%, NEXT 20% and small cap to denote each layer;

  • step2 Calculate the intersection between 5 size ranking layers and 5 IVOL ranking layers each month, and then get 5*5=25 portfolios, both value and equally weighted.

  • step3 Repeat step3 and step4 in part b.

Fig.1 shows the sorting method I used based on Ang et al. (2006).

This approach can further apply to other factors such as: momentum, pe, pb and ps ratio and many more as single factor test for ruling out IVOL puzzle.

4. Primitive Results

By applying the methodology in section 3, I have following findings:

  • By applying Fama-Macbeth regression and Ang et al. (2006) 's approach, IVOL puzzle exists in China Stock market. The average slope for IVOL is negative, and when controlling by FF-3 and firm size, low IVOL firms tend to have higher abnormal returns for all portfolios except the smallest[^mark2].

[^mark2]: I do not know why the effect disappears, it may because of smaller firms have larger information asymmetry and the IVOL effects blends with other effects and thus disappear.

  • Momentum cannot help explain IVOL puzzle, it even strengthens IVOL puzzle in the lowest IVOL group.

  • Single mispricing measure cannot eliminate IVOL puzzle. Pe, pb and ps helped to reduce magnitude of IVOL puzzle. As a strengthen of Stambaugh et al.(2015) with financial factors, I also find that IVOL puzzle is strong in large pe(tend to be overvalued) group but weak in small pe group.

  • Weighting will affect the magnitude of abnormal returns but will not change the results. When controlling size effect, IVOL puzzle is more significant than value weighted.

The data I use for estimation IVOL is daily data from wind database, financial and size data are month frequented and also come from wind data base, ranging from 2005.1 - 2017.3.[^mark3]

[^mark3]: In the following I only test size(market value), momentum(1 month return), pe pb and ps ratio. The reason and motivation to do this is that all the data I use here is monthly frequented, but the six anomalies are quarterly reported, therefore I have not enough time to deal with such heavy work.

Conclusion, Flaws and Further Exploration

As conclusion, this paper adopts Ang et al.(2006) 's method and extends their analysis to financial accounting factors with different weighting regime in China's stock market. My results confirm existence of IVOL puzzle and further finds that size, momentum cannot allay the magnitude of puzzle,while pe, pb and ps ratio can reduce the degree of puzzle, nevertheless equally weighting does not change the result except ps ratio. Nevertheless, for large pe stocks(tend to be overpriced) IVOL puzzle disappear. This is consistent with Stambaugh Yu and Yuan (2015) 's theory.

As for flaws and future exploration, points are as following:

  • I need robust check on groupings layers, try 3, 8, 10, and 15 layers.
  • Trading dates requirement tend to have a large effect on sample size.
  • Profitability and accrual factors are quarterly data and scholars argue that *ST stocks and finance stocks should not be included in order to observe related anomalies.
  • Rank stocks by many measurements can be problematic sometimes such as many stocks has same degree of mispricing levels and make sample size very volatile, need to check.
  • Representatives of mispricing measurement should be tested.
  • $\alpha$ under FF-3 model are always positive, may due to many reasons: time horizon is special, FF-3 model is biased(Stambaugh and Yu, 2017).
